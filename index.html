🌐 Foreign Lobbying Disclosure (FARA)

Official government data

What this is: Under the Foreign Agents Registration Act (1938), anyone in the U.S. representing a foreign government, political party, or foreign-controlled entity for political or public-relations purposes must register with the Department of Justice and disclose who they represent.

Why it matters: This is the primary public record of who is being paid to influence U.S. policy, media, or public opinion on behalf of a foreign interest.

How to read it: "Active" means the registration is currently in effect. "Terminated" means the agent formally ended the relationship or stopped required activity — it does not by itself imply wrongdoing.

🏛️ Domestic Lobbying Disclosure (LDA)

Official government data

What this is: Under the Lobbying Disclosure Act, firms and individuals lobbying Congress or federal agencies on behalf of a client must register and file quarterly reports on their activity, issues, and compensation.

Why it matters: This is the primary public record of who is lobbying the federal government, for whom, and on what issues — separate from campaign contributions.

How to read it: A "registration" is a new lobbying relationship being disclosed. High filing volume in a period reflects normal quarterly reporting cycles, not a spike in lobbying itself.

💵 Campaign Finance / PAC Activity (FEC)

Official government data

What this is: Federal Election Commission records of political action committees (PACs), party committees, and Super PACs registered to raise and spend money in federal elections.

Why it matters: This is the primary record of how many organized money operations exist around federal campaigns, and how active they are.

How to read it: A "Super PAC" can raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures but cannot contribute directly to candidates. Counts below are activity counts (how many committees/reports exist), not dollar totals — see the note below.

📷 ALPR / "Flock" Camera Deployment

Open-source research — not official

What this is: A research-verified inventory of U.S. law enforcement agencies documented using Flock Safety automatic license-plate-reader (ALPR) cameras, compiled by EFF's Atlas of Surveillance project. Each entry is individually researched and cited to a news article, public record, or government document — not anonymous map tagging. A secondary, purely crowdsourced worldwide camera-tag count from OpenStreetMap is included below it for context.

Why it matters: ALPR networks are expanding across the U.S. largely without centralized public reporting, and have drawn scrutiny for sharing local camera data with federal immigration enforcement without cities' authorization. This is the most rigorously sourced public accounting of that expansion available outside government itself.

How to read it — important caveats: this is still not official government data — EFF is a privacy advocacy organization, and each entry reflects what their researchers found, not a mandatory disclosure. Coverage reflects research effort as much as reality, and the dataset is only as current as its last update (see below). Treat it as the best available public accounting, not a real-time or exhaustive one.
